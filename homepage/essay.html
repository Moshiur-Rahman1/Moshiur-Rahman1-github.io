From Roots to Riches: The Mosaic of My Identity

Tucked away in Staten Island, the Bosniak Islamic Center stands as more than just another building in New York City's sprawling landscape. It's a beacon for culture, drawing those seeking connection in an environment distant from their heritage. As a high school student feeling torn between two worlds, this mosque was more than just a spot for weekly prayers; it helped me figure out my place in the big puzzle of life in the U.S. I still remember the first time I walked in. The place was buzzing with a language that somewhat resembled what I've heard at home, but I didn't grasp all of it. There were other kids around my age, talking easily in both Bosnian and English, making it seem cool to be from two different worlds. But honestly, it was overwhelming, the sense of being out of sync in a place where I was supposed to naturally fit in.

Ramadan nights at the mosque brought this contrast into starker relief. Families coming together to eat after fasting, the amazing smell of meals made from home, and the regular sounds of prayer made a feeling of togetherness that I couldn't quite join in on. I was there, but not quite. Even with everyone happy and praying together, I somewhat felt like I was watching from way back, as if there was a shared understanding that everyone grasped except for me. But, you know, belonging sneaks up on you. It wasn't a grand epiphany, more like a collage of little moments. A shared laugh when a toddler took their first steps at the community hall, the quick exchange of understanding glances when the Imam shared a particularly touching teaching, or the silent companionship during evening prayers. Piece by piece, I found myself not just observing but participating.

The older people, always with a grin and a story to share, helped a lot. They'd talk about their younger days, how hard it was to start fresh here and yet not forget where we came from. Hearing them made me think that maybe my teen troubles aren't just random; they're part of something bigger that's tied to our roots. Eid celebrations were when this hit home the hardest. The Center, lit up with lights and vibrant colors, was filled with both laughs and prayers, like a worldwide gathering place. Right there, among the flowy dresses and tasty foods from distant places, I got it â€” the amazing mix of what we carry from our past and what we discover here, the 'us' that's more than just a simple tag.

When I think about it, the Bosniak Islamic Center wasn't really about just blending in. It taught me that 'belonging' isn't a final stop. It's the memories we share, the quiet moments, and the space we make for ourselves and friends. 'Home' isn't just your birthplace; it's where you feel truly at ease and find meaning. Standing in the courtyard now, the towers look like they're reaching for the clouds, linking my two lives. They remind me of coming together, the good in differences, and the strength we find when we're all in it together. In this story of moving, believing, and finding my spot, the Bosniak Islamic Center isn't just some place; it's the beat that ties everything, from where we were, to now, and what's ahead.

Now, as I walk through the hallways of the Bosniak Islamic Center during this time in my life, everything feels different. The walls, decorated with beautiful designs that remind me of a far-off place, feel like a friend's house where I've spent countless time. The sound of kids reciting the Quran is a comforting background to my thoughts. These sounds used to make me feel left out, but now they're a big part of my community and who I am. The language that used to be a challenge for me now rolls off my tongue. It's not perfect, but it shows how cultures can come together. It's in the greetings from older folks, the local news chats, and the big talks about global happenings where I didn't just learn new words. I picked up on the feelings, the passion, and the context that makes language come alive. It's not just about talking; it's about really getting what a community is all about. At the Center's potlucks, where Burek and burgers were both on the menu, I saw how our traditions met the modern world. There, surrounded by the sounds of music, dance, and tales from our history, I felt at home and realized these events weren't just for show; they were a call to join in and be part of the story. Every culture, I learned, changes and grows with each shared memory and story. The Center didn't just teach me to be proud of where I came from; it also showed me the importance of helping others. Whether we were tidying up Staten Island parks or joining city charity events, it wasn't because we had to; it was because we wanted to give back to the community. At the end of the day, we weren't only representing Bosnians or Muslims; we were showing the best of what New Yorkers could be, adding to the rich mix of the city that welcomed us.

As time passed, I found myself becoming more involved at the Bosniak Islamic Center. From sitting quietly in the back to actively participating in youth discussions and helping to organize events, I became a part of something bigger than myself. The Center, nestled in Staten Island, reflects the diversity and resilience that characterizes New York City itself. My experiences and growth at the mosque became intertwined with those around me, forming a rich mosaic of faith, culture, and community. Just like the city, the Center is a place where different cultures seamlessly blend and thrive. It highlighted that the true strength of a community lies not in uniformity of thought, but in a united purpose and shared experiences.
